6. Autocorrelation

Here, the input signal is multiplied by the time shifted version of the same signal, and then displayed on the time axis.

It might sound a bit complicated in the beginning- hence first the question, what do you actually get out of autocorrelation:

Thanks to autocorrelation, periodicities of a signal - and thus frequencies - can be calculated.

A guitar string has even more overtones in addition to the fundamental frequency (e.g. 110 Hz for the A string). These can be displayed particularly easily through FFT (see next section). Overtones actually compose the typical sound of a string instrument, such as ukulele, guitar, bass, etc. - a pure sine wave with the fundamental frequency would sound very sterile and artificial!

Let's look at the recording (amplitude) of a guitar string on the time axis:

Here you can clearly recognize zero crossings in different distances- i.e., a determination of the fundamental frequency will be rather difficult or even impossible.

And now the same input signal is correlated with itself:

Now you can recognize a distinct peak, which stands for the root. The two smaller peaks on the left side represent harmonics. As a general rule, however, the higher the measured frequency, the further the appropriate maximum will move to the left. You can try this out by yourself when tuning a string instrument.

Finally, a pure sine wave (440 Hz, concert pitch) correlated with itself:

Unlike guitar or ukulele strings, here you will see a very harmonious pattern with a uniformly sloping envelope curve. This means that there are no harmonics.
